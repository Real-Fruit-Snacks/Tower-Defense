1. Damage Type / Element System

How many elements exist? This defines the tower↔enemy counter matrix and visual language.

3 Elements (Rock-Paper-Scissors)

Three elements in a cycle: Thermal beats Kinetic, Kinetic beats Electric, Electric beats Thermal. Clean triangle. Plus Physical (neutral).

PROS

  • Instantly learnable — just 3 matchups
  • Perfect strategic triangle
  • 3 colors + neutral = clean visual language
  • Easiest to balance
  • Works well with 12-15 towers (4-5 per element)

CONS

  • Less depth per matchup
  • Can feel simplistic for hardcore players
  • Limited color variety on enemies

4 Elements (Dual Pairs)

Two opposing pairs: Fire↔Ice and Lightning↔Poison. Each is strong vs its opposite. Plus Physical (neutral). Classic RPG model.

PROS

  • Familiar to RPG/game players
  • 4 distinct colors = good variety
  • Pairs are intuitive (fire melts ice, etc)
  • Room for combo effects (fire+poison = explosion)
  • Sweet spot of depth vs complexity

CONS

  • Slightly harder to learn than 3
  • Need ~3 towers per element to fill roster
  • Pairs can feel disconnected from each other

6 Elements (Full Wheel)

Six elements in a wheel: Fire > Wind > Earth > Lightning > Water > Fire. Each strong vs next, weak vs previous. Maximum depth.

PROS

  • Maximum strategic depth
  • Rich color palette for enemies
  • Every wave feels different
  • Lots of combo potential

CONS

  • Hard to memorize all matchups
  • Only ~2 towers per element with 12 roster
  • Most complex to balance
  • Can overwhelm new players
  • 6 colors on dark bg gets noisy

2. Tower Roster Concept

The specific tower lineup — roles, archetypes, and how they fill the roster. Click to select a roster approach.

Role-Based Roster (12 towers)

Towers organized by role: 4 DPS, 3 Control, 2 Support, 2 Economy, 1 Ultimate. Each role has distinct visual shape language. Clear team-building.

PROS

  • Clear roles — players know what to build
  • Every tower has a purpose
  • Easy to balance (buff roles, not individuals)
  • Natural "team comp" discussions

CONS

  • Roles can feel restrictive
  • Some roles may be "must pick"
  • Less room for weird/niche towers

Element-Spread Roster (12 towers)

Towers organized by element first: 3 per element (assuming 4 elements). Each element has a DPS, an AoE, and a utility variant. Element mastery matters.

PROS

  • Strong element identity
  • Natural counter-play with enemy colors
  • Element combos and synergies
  • Branching upgrades feel distinct per element

CONS

  • Same-element towers may feel similar
  • Forces element choice early in run
  • Less role diversity

Hybrid Roster (14 towers)

Mix of both: 8 elemental towers (2 per element, each a different role) + 4 neutral utility towers (slow, buff, economy, ultimate). Best of both.

PROS

  • Maximum build diversity
  • Element AND role identity
  • Neutral towers ensure utility in any build
  • 14 towers is the sweet spot

CONS

  • Most towers to create and balance
  • Players may ignore neutral towers
  • Complex unlock tree needed

3. Campaign Structure

How the campaign is organized — worlds, maps, and progression flow.

Linear Worlds (4 × 5)

4 themed worlds, each with 5 maps + a boss map. Play in order. Each world introduces a new element and enemy type. Clear 20-map campaign.

PROS

  • Clean progression arc
  • Each world = tutorial for one element
  • Easy to pace difficulty
  • 20 maps is a solid campaign length
  • Boss fights as milestones

CONS

  • No player choice in order
  • Can feel like a grind if stuck
  • Replayability depends on star system

Branching World Map

A branching map like Slay the Spire or FTL. Choose your path through nodes — some are battles, some are shops, some are elite/boss encounters.

PROS

  • Player agency in route choice
  • Natural roguelite fit (different each run)
  • Risk/reward paths (hard = better rewards)
  • Shops/events break up combat

CONS

  • More complex map generation
  • Harder to control difficulty curve
  • Some paths may be clearly better
  • No "world" theme identity

Worlds + Branching Paths

4 themed worlds with a branching node map inside each. Beat any path through a world to unlock the next. Combines structure with choice.

PROS

  • World themes give identity
  • Branching adds replayability within each world
  • Player choice without losing structure
  • Can replay worlds with different paths

CONS

  • Most content to design
  • Two layers of map UI needed
  • Balance across paths within a world

4. Audio Direction

The soundscape — music style, SFX approach, and overall audio identity.

Synthwave / Retrowave

Pulsing synth basslines, arpeggiated melodies, retro drum machines. Think Hotline Miami, Furi, or Carpenter Brut. High energy, driving rhythm.

PROS

  • Perfect match for cyberpunk neon visuals
  • High energy keeps gameplay exciting
  • Very popular / trendy audio style
  • Great for boss fight escalation
  • Lots of royalty-free options available

CONS

  • Can get repetitive in long sessions
  • Loud/busy mix may compete with SFX
  • Oversaturated in indie game market

Ambient Electronic

Atmospheric pads, subtle beats, evolving textures. Think FTL, Into the Breach, or Stellaris. Calm but tense. Builds with wave intensity.

PROS

  • Non-fatiguing for long sessions
  • SFX stand out clearly over ambient
  • Can dynamically shift with gameplay
  • Sophisticated, premium feel

CONS

  • Less immediately exciting
  • Boss fights may lack punch
  • Can feel too calm for action
  • Harder to make memorable

DnB / Aggressive Electronic

Fast breakbeats, heavy bass, glitchy textures. Think Geometry Wars, Beat Hazard, or Thumper. Maximum intensity. Matches abstract geometric theme.

PROS

  • Matches geometric/abstract perfectly
  • Incredible energy for action
  • Unique for tower defense genre
  • Great for "flow state" gameplay

CONS

  • Very niche taste — polarizing
  • Exhausting for casual players
  • Loudness competes with game SFX
  • Not everyone's cup of tea

Adaptive Layered (Dynamic)

Music that changes based on game state. Calm ambient during build phase → adds beats during waves → full intensity during boss/late waves. All electronic.

PROS

  • Most immersive — music matches action
  • Never too loud or too quiet
  • Build phase feels calm, waves feel tense
  • Professional AAA technique
  • Best of all other options combined

CONS

  • Most complex to implement
  • Need multiple layers per track
  • Crossfading / syncing is tricky
  • More audio assets needed

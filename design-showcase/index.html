1. Art Style

Defines the entire visual identity of the game and impacts development complexity.

Pixel Art 2D

Retro-inspired sprites on a tile grid. Charming, nostalgic aesthetic with strong visual clarity. Think Kingdom Rush or classic GBA-era games.

PROS

  • Assets are faster to create
  • Iconic, timeless look
  • Small file sizes
  • Easy to animate (frame-by-frame)
  • Large community asset packs available

CONS

  • Can look "indie" / less polished
  • Scaling requires nearest-neighbor
  • UI text clarity at low res
  • Hard to convey subtle effects
Best for: Solo devs. Fast iteration, strong identity with minimal art skill.

Vector / Clean 2D

Smooth, scalable vector-style graphics with modern flat design. Clean lines, bold colors, and crisp UI. Think Bloons TD 6 or modern mobile games.

PROS

  • Resolution-independent (any screen)
  • Modern, professional feel
  • Great for UI/UX polish
  • Smooth tweened animations
  • Works beautifully on mobile

CONS

  • Requires stronger art/design skill
  • Assets take longer to polish
  • Can feel generic without style
  • Larger sprite sheets
Best for: Polished commercial feel. Requires more art investment upfront.

2.5D Isometric

Isometric projection with depth and layering. Gives a sense of 3D space while staying 2D. Think classic SimCity, Mindustry, or Clash of Clans.

PROS

  • Impressive visual depth
  • Natural tower height hierarchy
  • Stands out from flat 2D games
  • Great for terrain variety

CONS

  • Complex coordinate math (iso grid)
  • Sorting/depth ordering is hard
  • Every asset needs iso perspective
  • Click detection more complex
  • Significantly more dev time
Best for: Visually ambitious projects with more dev time budget.

3D (WebGL / Three.js)

Full 3D rendering with camera control, real lighting, and model-based assets. Most visually impressive option. Think Element TD or Sanctum.

PROS

  • Most visually impressive
  • Real lighting and shadows
  • Camera rotation/zoom
  • Easiest to add VFX (particles etc)

CONS

  • Needs 3D modeling skills or assets
  • Much higher performance cost
  • Longer dev cycle
  • Harder to optimize for low-end
  • Mobile support is tricky
Best for: Teams with 3D experience, or if 3D is a core selling point.

2. Theme / Setting

The world, enemies, and narrative wrapper for your game.

Classic Fantasy

Medieval setting with castles, archers, mages, orcs, and dragons. The genre standard. Players immediately understand the world.

PROS

  • Instantly familiar to players
  • Tons of free/cheap art assets
  • Natural enemy variety (undead, beasts, demons)
  • Tower types write themselves (archer, mage, cannon)

CONS

  • Very crowded market — hard to stand out
  • Can feel generic without unique twist
  • Player expectations are high (Kingdom Rush exists)

Sci-Fi / Space

Lasers, drones, alien invasions, tech trees. Futuristic aesthetic with energy-based weapons and shield mechanics.

PROS

  • Unique tower mechanics (shields, EMP, hacking)
  • Great for particle effects and VFX
  • Less saturated than fantasy
  • Natural tech-tree progression

CONS

  • Fewer available art assets
  • Sci-fi art is harder to execute well
  • Enemy variety requires more creativity

Modern Military

Realistic-ish military setting. Gun turrets, missile launchers, tanks, infantry. Grounded aesthetic with ballistic mechanics.

PROS

  • Grounded, easy to understand
  • Real-world weapon references
  • Appeals to strategy/mil-sim fans
  • Clear damage type hierarchy

CONS

  • Limited magical/fantasy mechanics
  • Narrower audience appeal
  • Realistic art is harder to do well
  • Less whimsy / charm

Abstract / Geometric

Neon shapes, geometric enemies, minimalist design. Focus on mechanics over narrative. Think Geometry Wars meets tower defense.

PROS

  • No complex art needed — shapes and colors
  • Unique aesthetic, stands out
  • Fastest to prototype
  • Focus entirely on gameplay
  • Neon/glow effects look great cheaply

CONS

  • Harder to build emotional connection
  • No narrative hook
  • Can feel cold or sterile
  • Some players skip "abstract" games

3. Map Design

How enemies navigate and where towers can be placed — the core strategic layer.

Fixed Paths

Enemies follow pre-defined winding routes. Towers go in designated spots or zones beside the path. Strategy is about which tower goes where.

PROS

  • Easier to balance and design levels
  • No pathfinding needed (simpler code)
  • Each map is a hand-crafted puzzle
  • Clear strategic choices

CONS

  • Less player creativity/expression
  • Can feel rigid
  • Replay value depends on tower variety
  • Less emergent gameplay

Open Field / Maze Building

Enemies pathfind from entry to exit. Players place towers anywhere to create mazes and force longer routes. Deep emergent strategy.

PROS

  • Massive player creativity
  • High replay value
  • Emergent strategies (maze optimization)
  • Players share maze designs

CONS

  • Requires real-time pathfinding (A* / flow fields)
  • Harder to balance (maze exploits)
  • Must prevent complete path blocking
  • New players can feel overwhelmed

Hybrid

Some maps have fixed paths with tower slots. Others open up for free placement. Combines hand-crafted design with player freedom.

PROS

  • Best of both worlds
  • Maps feel varied
  • Can ease players in (fixed → open)
  • Maximum design flexibility

CONS

  • Must build both systems
  • Double the balance work
  • Inconsistent player expectations
  • More complex map editor needed

4. Progression System

What keeps players coming back — how does the game reward continued play?

In-Match Only

All progression happens within a single game. You earn gold, build towers, upgrade them. Next game starts from scratch. Pure skill-based.

PROS

  • Simplest to implement and balance
  • Every match is a fair start
  • No grind or pay-to-win concerns
  • Focus on pure strategy

CONS

  • No long-term motivation loop
  • Players may lose interest faster
  • No sense of "building something"
  • Less content to discover

Meta-Progression (Campaign)

Persistent unlocks across play sessions: new towers, global upgrades, achievements, star ratings. Campaign map with progression gating.

PROS

  • Strong long-term engagement loop
  • Players feel rewarded over time
  • Natural content drip (unlock new stuff)
  • Star ratings add replay incentive

CONS

  • Must balance early vs late game
  • Risk of grindy feeling
  • More save-state management
  • Balance with unlocked towers is harder

Roguelite Elements

Each run offers randomized tower/ability picks. Earn persistent currency for permanent upgrades between runs. Every run feels different.

PROS

  • Highest replay value
  • Each run is unique
  • Strong "one more run" pull
  • Trending genre combination

CONS

  • Most complex to design and balance
  • Randomness can feel unfair
  • Needs large pool of towers/abilities
  • No hand-crafted campaign experience

5. Tower Roster Size

How many distinct tower types to design, balance, and create art for.

6–8 Towers

Focused roster where every tower is deeply unique with distinct upgrade paths. Quality over quantity. Think chess — few pieces, deep strategy.

PROS

  • Easier to balance perfectly
  • Less art/animation work
  • Every tower feels essential
  • Faster to ship v1

CONS

  • Less variety per match
  • Players see everything quickly
  • Fewer combo possibilities

12–15 Towers

Sweet spot roster with good variety. Multiple damage types, support towers, area control, economy towers. Room for interesting synergies.

PROS

  • Good variety without overwhelm
  • Room for niche/specialist towers
  • Strong synergy potential
  • Balanceable with effort

CONS

  • Moderate art investment
  • Some towers may feel redundant
  • Longer balance iteration

20+ Towers

Large roster for maximum variety and discovery. Works best with unlock systems so players don't see everything at once.

PROS

  • Maximum variety and discovery
  • Unlock progression feels rewarding
  • More build diversity
  • Community theorycrafting

CONS

  • Massive balance challenge
  • Many assets to create
  • Risk of filler/weak towers
  • UI/selection becomes complex

6. Tower Upgrade Structure

How towers evolve during a match — the micro-decision layer.

Linear Tiers (1→2→3→4)

Each tower upgrades straight through tiers. Each level is strictly better. Simple to understand, easy to balance. The classic approach.

PROS

  • Simplest to understand for players
  • Easiest to balance
  • Clear power curve
  • Clean UI — just a level number

CONS

  • No meaningful upgrade choices
  • Optimal play is always "upgrade everything"
  • Less strategic depth per tower
  • Can feel monotonous

Branching Paths

Each tower has 2–3 upgrade paths that diverge. Pick one branch to specialize. Same base tower, very different final forms. (Bloons TD style)

PROS

  • Meaningful choices per tower
  • Multiplies effective tower variety
  • Replay value (try different branches)
  • Proven popular model

CONS

  • More complex UI needed
  • 3x the upgrade content to design
  • Harder to balance all paths
  • One path often dominates

Modular / Component-Based

Towers have slots for swappable modules (damage type, firing pattern, special ability). Build your own tower configuration. Most player agency.

PROS

  • Maximum player expression
  • Enormous combinatorial depth
  • Unique — few TD games do this well
  • Module drops can feed roguelite loop

CONS

  • Very complex UI/UX challenge
  • Hardest to balance (combinatorial explosion)
  • Can overwhelm casual players
  • Most development work

7. Game Modes

What players can actually do — the content structure of the game.

Campaign Only

A curated series of maps with increasing difficulty, star ratings, and unlock gates. Focused, polished experience with clear start and end.

PROS

  • Focused development effort
  • Hand-tuned difficulty curve
  • Clear sense of progress
  • Best for storytelling

CONS

  • Finite content — players finish it
  • Less replay value
  • No sandbox/chill mode

Campaign + Endless

Campaign for structure and unlocks, plus an endless/survival mode on each map for high-score chasing and testing builds.

PROS

  • Campaign gives structure
  • Endless gives infinite replayability
  • Caters to both casual and hardcore
  • Endless mode is relatively cheap to add

CONS

  • Endless needs separate balance pass
  • Late endless can expose balance issues
  • Need scaling system for waves

Campaign + Endless + Challenges

All modes plus rotating challenge modifiers: limited towers, fast enemies, restricted budget, special rules. Keeps things fresh weekly.

PROS

  • Maximum content variety
  • Challenges create "events"
  • Tests player mastery
  • Community engagement driver

CONS

  • Most content to build and maintain
  • Challenge modifiers need a rules engine
  • Rotating content needs a schedule system
  • Risk of spreading effort too thin
